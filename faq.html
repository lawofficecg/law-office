Common questions about slip-and-fall, trip-and-fall, and other premises liability matters in Southern California.

You are not required to hire a lawyer, but talking to one early can help protect evidence and your rights. Conditions get fixed, surveillance footage gets deleted, and witnesses become harder to find as time passes. A free consultation can help you understand whether a property owner may be responsible and what your options are. See our Personal Injury practice area for more detail.

Seek medical attention if you're injured, then document the scene as soon as you safely can. Take photos of the hazard and the surrounding area, get the names and contact information of any witnesses, and report the incident to the property owner, manager, or business if possible. Keep a record of any medical treatment you receive. If you have questions about next steps, you can contact our office directly.

The most useful evidence shows that a hazardous condition existed and that the property owner knew or should have known about it. This can include photos or video of the condition, an incident report, contact information for witnesses, maintenance or complaint records, and your own medical records documenting the injury.

Responsibility can fall on the property owner, a business operating on the premises, a tenant in possession of the property, or a property management company, depending on who controlled the area and the condition involved. California law generally requires those in control of a property to maintain it in a reasonably safe condition and to address hazards they knew or should have known about.

These are categories of damages that may be available in a personal injury claim, depending on the facts of your case. What can actually be recovered depends on liability, available insurance, and the specific circumstances of your situation, so we cannot promise a particular outcome or amount without first reviewing your case.

Not reporting immediately does not automatically prevent you from pursuing a claim, but reporting and documenting the incident as soon as possible generally strengthens it. If you have not already reported the incident, you can still do so now, and you can gather whatever evidence is still available.

A case can still be possible without video footage or witnesses, though those forms of evidence are helpful when they exist. Photos taken after the fact, incident reports, maintenance or complaint history, and medical records can also support a claim. An attorney can help evaluate what evidence is realistically available in your situation.

Common questions about mold, water intrusion, and other unsafe rental conditions under California habitability law.

California law allows tenants to pursue legal claims against landlords who fail to address mold and other conditions that make a rental unit unsafe to live in. Under the implied warranty of habitability, landlords must maintain rental properties in livable condition, and a landlord who ignores a documented mold problem after proper notice may be legally responsible. See our Habitability Law practice area for more detail.

Send written notice describing the problem and requesting repairs, and keep a copy of everything you send and receive. Document the condition with dated photos and video, and consider getting a mold or air test done. If your landlord continues to ignore the issue after proper notice, you may have grounds for a legal claim.

The most useful evidence includes dated photos and videos of the conditions, written communication with your landlord, and any test results you have. A clear timeline of when the problem started, when you reported it, and how your landlord responded is also valuable. Our Resources page has a practical guide to documenting habitability issues.

An air or mold test is required. Testing helps confirm the presence and extent of mold, which can support both a legal claim and decisions about your health and safety.

If you're experiencing symptoms you believe may be related to mold exposure, you should see a medical provider. We are not able to diagnose a medical condition or confirm a medical link between your symptoms and mold exposure — that determination belongs to a qualified healthcare provider. Documenting your symptoms and any medical treatment you receive can still be relevant to a habitability case.

Conditions like mold, water intrusion, sewage backups, and broken heat, plumbing, or electrical systems can make a unit legally uninhabitable under California law. California's habitability standards generally require landlords to maintain rental housing that is safe, sanitary, and structurally sound.

What a tenant may recover depends on the specific facts of the case, including the conditions involved, how the landlord responded, and the impact on the tenant. We cannot guarantee a particular result, but an attorney can review your situation and explain what may realistically be available.

Mold and habitability cases are handled on a contingency-fee basis: no attorney’s fees unless there is a recovery. Case costs are addressed separately and explained before representation begins. You can submit an intake form to start a free consultation.
